What we do!

Connect with other families for mutual support and education Share our experience of what worked and what did not for us and our loved ones Foster a dialogue between family members and their loved ones diagnosed with mental illness Fight stigma and discrimination Share information about evidence-based treatment methods Share tips for choosing the mental health provider and navigating the mental health system based on our personal experience Share tips about reading and understanding medical literature based on our personal experience Provide a space for uncensored conversation for everyone interested in the subject of mental health Welcome members of all faith and religious backgrounds, as well as atheists Collect personal stories (the position of the authors does not necessarily have to reflect the position of the FAMHR)

What we don't do?

Take money from pharmaceutical companies and producers of medical devices Participate in terminological controversies (debates over the best words to use) Give medical advice “Pill shaming” Promote treatments that are not supported by scientific evidence (such treatments may be mentioned in personal stories) Promote spiritual interpretations of experiences commonly described as “psychosis” (such interpretations may be found in personal stories) Give advice on how to make somebody adhere to a treatment they do not want or how to commit somebody to a hospital or Assisted Outpatient Treatment against their will Have affiliation with any religious or political group

Mission statement

"Family Alliance for Mental Health Recovery provides mutual support for people seeking mental health recovery for themselves and their loved ones and promotes humane, wellness-oriented care."

Core Message

A psychiatric diagnosis does NOT mean changing one's life plans, abandoning one's dreams, inability to have family, and living as a chronically ill or disabled person. Recovery happens more often than most people realize. Family support plays a key role.

Information for Family Members

As one parent to another, if your loved one perceives reality in a very unusual way - hears voices that other people don't hear, sees things that others don't see, and/or has other uncommon experiences - this is conventionally called psychosis or “psychotic episode.” When your kid starts talking with invisible aliens, or claims that the CIA implanted a device in their brain, it can be scary for you, but try not to panic. Remember that voices, visions and unusual beliefs and perceptions do not, in themselves, make somebody more violent than the average person. Stay calm and compassionate, find the right kind of help that your child will be willing to accept, and this episode most likely will end with no lasting damage. You and your child may even learn something important from this experience.

If your loved one receives a diagnosis of serious mental illness, such as “schizophrenia,” “bipolar disorder,” “major depression,” or “obsessive compulsive disorder,” provide them with support and advocacy in their journey to recovery. Many people with such diagnoses live a “normal” life - receive professional degrees, have careers and families (and no, their children have no special risk of getting some “terrible disease” or “bad genes”). We hear little about good outcomes for people diagnosed with SMI, because most of them stay silent about their experiences, fearing stigma and discrimination. Read inspiring stories of successful people with SMI diagnoses who courageously came out of the closet.
