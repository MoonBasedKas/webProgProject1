What we do!

Connect with other families for mutual support and education Share our experience of what worked and what did not for us and our loved ones Foster a dialogue between family members and their loved ones diagnosed with mental illness Fight stigma and discrimination Share information about evidence-based treatment methods Share tips for choosing the mental health provider and navigating the mental health system based on our personal experience Share tips about reading and understanding medical literature based on our personal experience Provide a space for uncensored conversation for everyone interested in the subject of mental health Welcome members of all faith and religious backgrounds, as well as atheists Collect personal stories (the position of the authors does not necessarily have to reflect the position of the FAMHR)

What we don't do?

Take money from pharmaceutical companies and producers of medical devices Participate in terminological controversies (debates over the best words to use) Give medical advice “Pill shaming” Promote treatments that are not supported by scientific evidence (such treatments may be mentioned in personal stories) Promote spiritual interpretations of experiences commonly described as “psychosis” (such interpretations may be found in personal stories) Give advice on how to make somebody adhere to a treatment they do not want or how to commit somebody to a hospital or Assisted Outpatient Treatment against their will Have affiliation with any religious or political group

Participate in terminological controversies

“Mental illness” and “mental disorder” are controversial terms. Some people embrace them; some think that they are not valid concepts; some find them stigmatizing. Some embrace the term “madness” and describe themselves as “mad” rather than “mentally ill”; some apply the broad term “psychosocial disability” to what others would describe as “madness,” “mental illness,” or “extreme state.” Among those who reject the concept of “mental illness/disorder,” some accept terms like “psychosis,” “delusions,” “hallucinations”; others use different words and expressions for the states conventionally described by these terms.

We follow the SAMHSA Guide to Equity Terminology: “Acknowledge and respect how people choose to define and identify themselves.” In our conversations, everyone is free to use the language of their choice without any censure. For example, in our personal stories, one author may talk about “psychosis,” another about “an extreme state,” or about “hearing voices and seeing visions.” To describe your own experience on this website, feel free to use any terms you are comfortable with.

For the perspective of those embracing the term “mental illness,” see, for example, National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) and Mental Illness Policy. For the perspective of those rejecting the term “mental illness” and using different language, see, for example, ISEPP and MindFreedom.

For a better understanding of how different ways to describe one's psychological suffering affects the individual, see the story of Rose Cartwright who used to be “the poster girl for obsessive-compulsive disorder.”
